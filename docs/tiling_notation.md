# Schläfli Symbol and Tiling Notation

## The Schläfli Symbol {p,q}

The Schläfli symbol `{p,q}` describes a regular tiling (or polytope):
- `p`: number of sides of each polygon (p-gon)
- `q`: number of polygons meeting at each vertex

### Reading the Symbol

| Symbol | Geometry | Description |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| {3,3}  | Spherical | Tetrahedron (3 triangles at each vertex) |
| {4,3}  | Spherical | Cube (3 squares at each vertex) |
| {3,4}  | Spherical | Octahedron (4 triangles at each vertex) |
| {3,5}  | Spherical | Icosahedron (5 triangles at each vertex) |
| {5,3}  | Spherical | Dodecahedron (3 pentagons at each vertex) |
| {3,6}  | Euclidean | Triangular tiling (6 triangles at each vertex) |
| {4,4}  | Euclidean | Square tiling (4 squares at each vertex) |
| {6,3}  | Euclidean | Hexagonal tiling (3 hexagons at each vertex) |
| {5,4}  | Hyperbolic | Pentagonal tiling (4 pentagons at each vertex) |
| {7,3}  | Hyperbolic | Heptagonal tiling (3 heptagons at each vertex) |
| {∞,3}  | Hyperbolic | Apeirogonal tiling (3 apeirogons at each vertex) |

### The Hyperbolicity Condition

A tiling {p,q} is:
- **Spherical** if `1/p + 1/q > 1/2`
- **Euclidean** if `1/p + 1/q = 1/2`
- **Hyperbolic** if `1/p + 1/q < 1/2`

The critical equation comes from the angular defect. At each vertex, q polygons meet.
The interior angle of a regular p-gon (Euclidean) is `(p-2)π/p`. For the tiling
to close up flat: `q · (p-2)π/p = 2π`, i.e., `(p-2)/p · q = 2`, i.e., `1/p + 1/q = 1/2`.

Less than `1/2` means there is angular *excess* at each vertex, which requires negative curvature.

## Dual Tilings

The dual of {p,q} is {q,p}: swap the roles of faces and vertices.

For each face of {p,q}, place a vertex in the center.
Connect centers of adjacent faces to get the dual tiling {q,p}.

| Tiling | Dual |
|--------|------|
| {7,3}  | {3,7} |
| {5,4}  | {4,5} |
| {6,4}  | {4,6} |
| {∞,3}  | {3,∞} |

Duals share the same triangle group (2,p,q) = (2,q,p) — just with p and q swapped.

## Extended Notation

### Quasiregular Tilings r{p,q}

A quasiregular tiling `r{p,q}` has two types of polygons alternating:
- p-gons and q-gons, alternating at each edge

The Schläfli symbol includes the vertex figure: a rectangle with side-types p and q.

### Truncated Tilings

- `t{p,q}` = truncated {p,q}: cut off each vertex, creating (2p)-gons and q-gons
- `t{q,p}` = truncated {q,p}: creates p-gons and (2q)-gons
- `tr{p,q}` = omnitruncated: all vertices removed, three different polygon types

In hyperbolic space, truncations give Archimedean-type tilings with two or three polygon types.

### Wythoff Construction

The Wythoff symbol `p|q r` describes tilings generated by placing a point in a triangle group:
- `p|q r`: point on the p-vertex → p-gon tiling {p,q}... (simplified)

Full Wythoff notation:
- `p q r|`: point at the incenter → largest polygon
- `2|p q`: point at the right-angle vertex → edge midpoints only

## Vertex Figures

The **vertex figure** of a tiling {p,q} is the q-gon seen at each vertex
(the cycle of p-gons around that vertex traces a regular q-gon in the
dual space).

For {7,3}: each vertex has 3 heptagons meeting — vertex figure is a triangle.
For {5,4}: each vertex has 4 pentagons meeting — vertex figure is a square.

## Star Polygons {p/k}

Star polygons use fractional notation: {p/k} connects every k-th vertex of a p-gon.

- {5/2} = pentagram (skip every other vertex of a pentagon)
- {7/2} = {7,2} heptagram
- {7/3} = {7,3} heptagram (sharper)

In hyperbolic tilings, {5/2, 5} denotes a star tiling where pentagrams tile the disk
with 5 meeting at each vertex. These are "density-k" tilings where the polygon
winds around its center multiple times.

## Apeirogons {∞}

The apeirogon {∞} is the degenerate case p → ∞: a regular polygon with infinitely
many sides, whose vertices lie on a horocycle.

- {∞,3} = apeirogonal tiling: three apeirogons at each vertex
- In the Poincaré disk, the apeirogon appears as a region between two geodesics
  meeting at an ideal point, bounded by a horocycle

## Hyperbolic vs. Euclidean Tilings

### Density and Packing

In Euclidean {6,3} (honeycomb): 3 hexagons per vertex.
In hyperbolic {6,4}: 4 hexagons per vertex — possible only because hyperbolic space
allows more polygons at a vertex without exceeding 2π of total angle.

### Growth Rate

In Euclidean tilings: the number of tiles within hyperbolic distance d grows as d².
In hyperbolic tilings: the number of tiles grows exponentially in d.
This exponential growth is why the boundary appears infinitely dense.

### Infinite Volume

Every non-compact hyperbolic tiling (like {7,3} in ℍ²) has infinite total volume.
But each individual tile has **finite** area. There are countably infinite tiles,
each indistinguishable from the others.
